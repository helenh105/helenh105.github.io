# A brief introduction of water quality

This report is a brief overview of the amount of lead contamination in water and how it affects water quality in the United States. 

## Background

Lead contamination in water is a serious issue that leads to health issues and affects across people of all ages. 

Lead commonly finds its way into water by the pipes that it travels through in households as a result of corrosion, which is the breakdown of piping material that leads to structural breakdowns, water contamination, and leaks. [[1]](./writeup-sources#source-1) 
[[2]](./writeup-sources#source-2) 


This can be a problem towards households who use tap water as a drinking source or in cooking as well as ones that were built before 1986. 

# Why Lead?

The dataset used provides information on both copper and lead contamination into water and how its quality is affected, but it requires less concentrations of it to have effects on the body. Additionally, according to the CDC and EPA there is no known safe level of lead in a child’s blood which makes this issue important to be aware of especially for large sized households that include children and infants. [[1]](./writeup-sources#source-1) 

# Taking Action -- Lead and Copper Rule

In general there is no safe amount of lead, so to help this awareness be more widespread the United States Environmental Protection Agency imposed the Lead and Copper Rule (LCR) in 1991. The LCR is a regulation used to inform the public of when copper levels reach 1.3 mg/L and lead levels reach 0.015 mg/L. [[2]](./writeup-sources#source-2) 

This informs residents of when water quality levels of lead and copper reach an amount that will require action to be taken in regards to protecting themselves.
